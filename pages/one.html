Tuner Culture

Its 2000. You put down the new issue of Vogue and call your friend on your nokia.
You own a 1993 Honda Civic coupe. Its got every hallmark of a family car. Spacious, efficient on fuel, light and cheap to maintain.
The most a cheap commuter car has been at this time (especially imported ones from europe or japan) is transport. American muscle cars and Japanese rally monsters are the shining gem of the racing world. Races at this time usually focused on sheer power above all else.

Your car is hiding a secret: This year, you will sell it for something new. It will be picked up from the car auction by a movie producer, painted black, and fitted with a green underglow. Just a year later, your car will hijack trucks, peform stunts and jumps, and star in a blockbuster film.

Car Feature: 1993 Honda Civic Coupe

In Fast & Furious, main character Dom Toretto and his crew race 3 legendary Honda Civic Coupes in order to steal palettes of CD players to make money. In an iconic move, one slides under the truck, maintaining perfect speed as it nestles itself under the 6-wheelers cargo. With a green underglow and aggressive styling, though this car doesn't have the full hero car status of Brian's Toyota Supra, it demonstrates an ability to truly 'wow' audiences with well done precision driving and stunts. Despite the looks, these cars were built cheap, with used parts from Ebay, and all loud engine noises were edited in post. In reality, the Civic was still the same car it is today: reliable, cost-efficient, and slow. Very, very slow.

Rooted in the Streets

As usual, things began with young people getting their hands on something they probably shouldn't have. The most accessible cars were cheap imported compact cars. Presented by magazines such as Import Racer! and Option, posters of cars such as the Honda Fit, Nissan 220Z, and Mitsubishi Lancer filled the newly burgeoning scene. Its hard to pinpoint the exact origin of Tuners, but the SoCal scene is a good start. Tired of the old-fashioned and oftentimes exclusive American muscle scene, young adults looked towards imports for new fun. As usual, the first races with tuners were absolutely, wholeheartedly, illegal. Without a standard to meet or rules for safety, the implication was clear: do whatever makes the car go faster and look cool, for the cheapest price.

Changing the market

The shift towards import cars didn't go unnoticed. Japanese brands were quick to cash in. Once JDM-only brands, such as Mugen and Greddy began to advertise their parts in American magazines. Some even opened shops in California and Florida, attempting to become brand names in America during the new trend. In turn, these parts became cheaper and more accessible, further increasing the popularity of Japanese parts. It was a positive feedback loop where American brands had failed: where they had focused on providing intense power with massive, costly engines or hand-made modifications, Japanese parts provided a wider variety of small, performance enhancing upgrades on already existing engines, such as Hondas famous K-series engines.

Hey! What about Europe?

Quietly, Europe was making its way down the scene. Though it didn't have the variety of aftermarket parts that Japanese producers provided, makes such as BMW and Porsche saw the potential in producing for younger markets. In exchange for a lack of customizability, European makes provided quirky, unique designs with classic engines. This era saw some of the strangest, most bubble-shaped concept cars as they tried to match the quirkiness of homemade customized cars.

Building your Tuner

Lets put together a period accurate tuner, part by part.

The Car

When thinking tuner, think: It's cheap, but reliable. You might be enticed by the reliability of Toyota, or the incredibly popular engines of Honda cars. You may want the newly developed All-Wheel drive technology of Subaru, or the potential in a low-end BMW. For this example, lets pick a Manual 2002 Honda Fit. Its tiny, with about 110 horsepower.

The Engine

Popular modifications included the Turbocharger, a peformance air intake, and new valve covers. All of these have the intent of boosting the efficiency and power of your engine at once. More bang for your buck! As we're on a budget, lets do one of the cheapest modification to the engine at the time, changing the spark plugs with a performance alternative. After about 40 minutes and $50, your engine has gained a miniscule 8 horsepower.

Exhaust

Every sporty car sounds a little loud. So, lets make your Honda Fit match. A common modification at the time was removing the muffler, which is illegal in many provinces and states. Since we don't have the intention of making a road legal car, lets perform what's called a muffler delete.

Wheels and Suspension

In the height of tuner culture, wheels were mostly an accessory and for looks. Catalogues focused on small, affordable upgrades to engines or visual appearance. However, used parts were booming and the up and coming Ebay provided a massive selection at staggeringly cheap prices. Lets buy used rims, which auctioned at about $250 for a set at the time. Since they are lighter, your car has gained power and efficiency.

The real 'Tune'

You might still be wondering what exactly 'tuning' refers to. In the modern day, the term has become synonymous with engine tuning, the process of connecting to the cars computer with a laptop, and modifying its settings and which impacts how it behaves and controls the engine. You may recall a trope you've seen in movies, with a street-wise kid driving an old, seemingly harmless car with a laptop on the passenger seat.

This process is extremely delicate, (Yes, it can and will kill your engine permanently if not done correctly.) and requires an experienced technician or mechanic. Luckily, companies produced USB Sticks that automatically complete the tuning process based on the engine. You use the HONDATA stick, which is still used to this day to tune Hondas.
