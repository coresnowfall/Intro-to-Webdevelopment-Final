All About Graphics

Graphics were a product of the main audience of tuner culture. Since every car was uniquely modified, and had different aftermarket parts, it was a race to impress with increasingly crazier and brighter looks. As a result, the tuners we know and love were comprised of a variety of bright colours, flames, intricate designs, and customized 3D work. Anything, and everything was put in a car.

The Livery

Pronounced liv-er-ee, the Livery was the core of any cars looks. They are made up of large, sweeping designs on the cars exterior that attempt to capture the natural movement and shape of the car. Liveries could be applied with a wrap, spray-painted on, painted by brush, or applied as stickers. Due to the limits on manufacturing of stickers at the time, it was often the case that every car Livery was a unique piece of art.

Tuners in Media

Accurate? Somewhat. An exaggeration? Absolutely.

Car Feature: Suki's Honda S2000

In Fast & Furious 2, we see a wide variety of colourful and bright cars. A fan favourite is Suki's Honda S2000, which features bright pink, fine details such as bubbles, sparkles and a spray painted Livery of an anime girl. This Livery was done by spray-paint artist Noah Elias for 11 thousand US dollars. In fact, this car is reused several times within the film anthology just to save money for expensive paint jobs. In the modern era, this car continues to be replicated and remade by car enthusiasts hoping to capture the magic of Fast & Furious's tuners. Sold at auction after filming of the first 3 movies in the series ended, this car fetched a head-turning 24 thousand US dollars.

You may recall the tuners from Cars, which prey on a truck dozing off. They sported bright flames, custom parts, underglows, and large body kits. Depictions made the publics impression clear: a great tuner is as good as its looks. In turn, the more media depicted cars with aggresive styling, bright colours, and crazy paint jobs, the more enthusiasts wanted to replicate that look. It was a feedback loop, in which crazier and more unique cars always got more and more attention. You may recall MTV's Cribs, in which celebrities displayed their highly modified cars. Cars with body kits and modifications that cost the same as a new car were shown off, for example Shaq O' Niel's custom stretched Lamborghini Gallardo, made to fit his tall body comfortably despite the car being extremely low.

Another notable depiction of tuners is the impound cars from Fast & Furious 2. In the movie, undercover agent Brian is provided a lot of impound cars to drive with, all of which bear classic tuner looks. The movie seeked to depict tuners as a forbidden, sealed away item. Too dangerous to not keep caged. As a result, enthusiasts to this day attempted to recreate the looks of the cars in movies. This lead to an increase in painting skills and creativity, which then led to a livery renaissance in the early 2000s as inspired artists contributed to a growing community.
Overall, depictions of tuners were a mixed bag. They were an exaggeration of a newly-budding culture, but inspired that culture in turn through their exaggeration.
